Bank Marketing Campaign Analysis with Clustering


Introduction
Based on the Kaggle dataset available in Predicting-Portuguese-Bank-Term Deposit, this case study focuses on applying clustering techniques to analyze data from a Portuguese banking institution's direct marketing campaigns. The goal is to segment clients into distinct groups based on various attributes such as demographic information and campaign data. By clustering the customers, we aim to identify patterns and insights that can help improve marketing strategies and better understand customer behavior.

Objective
The objective of this study is to apply clustering techniques to segment clients into distinct groups based on attributes such as demographic information and campaign data. The aim is to identify patterns within the data that can help understand customer behavior and tailor marketing strategies more effectively. By clustering the clients, we seek to uncover hidden structures in the dataset, allowing for better targeting of future campaigns.


Development
Analysis of attributes and their distributions:
  • Age: Represents the user's age.
    The 'Age' feature is an integer value representing the age of the customer. It is a numeric attribute with no missing values.
  • Job: Represents the user's occupation.
    The 'Job' feature is categorical, containing various occupation types such as 'admin.', 'blue-collar', 'entrepreneur', 'housemaid', 'management', 'retired', 'self-employed', 'services', 'student', 'technician', 'unemployed', 'unknown'. The distribution of jobs is varied across the dataset. Missing values are present in this feature.
  • Marital Status: Represents the marital status of the user.
    The 'Marital Status' feature is categorical, with values such as 'divorced', 'married', 'single', and 'unknown'. This attribute describes the user's marital situation.
  • Education Level: Represents the user's level of education.
    The 'Education' feature is categorical, with values like 'basic.4y', 'basic.6y', 'basic.9y', 'high.school', 'illiterate', 'professional.course', 'university.degree', and 'unknown'. The dataset shows a range of education levels among customers. There are missing values in this feature.
  • Credit Default: Indicates whether the user has any credit in default.
    The 'Default' feature is binary, where 'yes' indicates a credit default, and 'no' indicates no credit default. This feature helps in assessing the financial risk of a customer.
  • Balance: Represents the average yearly balance in euros.
    The 'Balance' feature is a numeric integer representing the average yearly balance of the customer in euros. It is a key indicator of a customer's financial situation.
  • Housing Loan: Indicates whether the user has a housing loan.
    The 'Housing' feature is binary, where 'yes' indicates the user has a housing loan, and 'no' indicates they do not. This is important for understanding the user's financial obligations.
  • Personal Loan: Indicates whether the user has a personal loan.
    The 'Loan' feature is binary, with 'yes' representing a customer who has a personal loan and 'no' representing a customer without one. It is an important factor in evaluating customer debt.
  • Contact Communication Type: Represents the type of communication used to contact the customer.
    The 'Contact' feature is categorical, with values like 'cellular' and 'telephone'. It identifies the method used to reach the customer during the marketing campaign. Note that this feature has missing values.
  • Last Contact Day of Week: Represents the day of the week when the last contact was made.
    The 'Day_of_Week' feature is a date attribute that indicates the day of the week when the most recent contact took place. It helps to analyze the impact of specific days on customer responses.
  • Last Contact Month: Represents the month when the last contact was made.
    The 'Month' feature is categorical and indicates the month in which the last contact was made. This helps identify seasonal patterns in customer behavior.
  • Last Contact Duration: Represents the duration of the last contact in seconds.
    The 'Duration' feature is numeric and measures the length of the last call in seconds. It has a strong correlation with the target variable, as longer calls may indicate a higher likelihood of subscription.
  • Campaign Contacts: Represents the number of contacts made during the campaign.
    The 'Campaign' feature is a numeric value representing the number of contacts made for a client during the current campaign. This is an important indicator of customer engagement.
  • Days Since Last Contact: Represents the number of days since the client was last contacted in a previous campaign.
    The 'Pdays' feature is numeric and measures the time passed since the client's last contact in a prior campaign. This can influence the likelihood of success in current marketing efforts.
  • Previous Contacts: Represents the number of contacts made before the current campaign.
    The 'Previous' feature is numeric and indicates how many contacts were made to the customer before the current campaign. This attribute provides insight into the customer's prior engagement with the bank.
  • Outcome of Previous Campaign: Represents the outcome of the last marketing campaign.
    The 'Poutcome' feature is categorical, with possible values like 'failure', 'nonexistent', and 'success'. This helps assess the effectiveness of prior marketing efforts. This feature also has missing values.

    • Clustering Analysis with K-means

    Process created using clustering techniques, specifically K-means, to segment clients into distinct groups based on their attributes.

    1. Data Cleaning: The first step involves cleaning the dataset. Columns with many missing values, such as 'poutcome' and 'contact', are removed, along with special identifier columns like 'ID'. Missing values in categorical columns such as 'job' and 'education' are imputed using the mode (most frequent value). In addition, outliers are detected and removed using K-means clustering. Data points that deviate significantly from the cluster centroids are considered outliers and excluded from the analysis to improve clustering accuracy and avoid distortion.



    2. Data Preprocessing: Categorical variables are encoded into numerical values using Label Encoding. This allows the K-means clustering algorithm to work with them, as it requires numerical data. Additionally, the features are normalized using Min-Max Scaling to bring them to a common scale, ensuring that variables with larger ranges do not dominate the clustering process. This step is crucial for the algorithm's performance, as K-means is sensitive to the scale of the data. The target variable is not used in clustering, as clustering does not require a target variable.


    3. K-means Model Training and Evaluation: The K-means clustering algorithm was applied to partition the data into clusters. Initially, different values for the number of clusters were tested, and the Elbow Method was used to identify the optimal number of clusters. This method involves plotting the inertia (sum of squared distances from each point to its assigned centroid) against the number of clusters. The "elbow" point, where the inertia starts decreasing at a slower rate, indicates the best number of clusters. In this case, the optimal number of clusters identified by the Elbow Method was 4.



    4. Model Evaluation and Cluster Stability: After determining the optimal number of clusters, the stability of the clusters was evaluated by running the K-means algorithm multiple times with different initializations. This process ensures that the model does not converge to local minima, and the cluster results remain consistent. We used the Silhouette Score to assess the quality of the clusters, which measures the cohesion and separation of the clusters. A higher Silhouette Score indicates better-defined clusters.


    5. Evaluation Metrics: To evaluate the clustering performance, the Silhouette Score was calculated. This metric measures the cohesion and separation of the clusters, helping assess the overall quality and appropriateness of the chosen number of clusters. A higher value indicates better-defined and well-separated clusters.


    By comparing the Silhouette Scores for different values of k (3, 4, and 5), and considering the visualizations, we can confirm the results from the Elbow Method. As we observed earlier, k = 4 provided the best balance between cluster cohesion and separation, making it the optimal choice for this dataset.


    6. Cluster Analysis: After obtaining the final clusters, the centroids of each cluster were analyzed to understand the characteristics of the groups. The distribution of features within each cluster provided insights into different customer segments, which can be useful for marketing strategy development and customer targeting.

    Clustering Results and Evaluation

    The clustering analysis revealed challenges in the definition of the clusters. The Siluette Score, used to evaluate cluster separation, was lower than expected, indicating that the clusters might overlap. Visual inspections of the cluster plots also revealed poor separation and indistinct boundaries.


    Conclusion

    The analysis of clustering results highlighted key limitations of the K-Means algorithm for this dataset. K-Means works under the assumption that clusters are spherical and of similar size, but the low Siluette Score and poor separation of clusters suggest this assumption did not hold in this case. Many data points seemed to belong to more than one cluster, reflecting weak cohesion and separation.

    These issues indicate that K-Means might not be well-suited for datasets with complex cluster structures, especially when clusters vary in shape or density. In such cases, K-Means often leads to overlapping clusters, which diminishes the overall quality of the segmentation.

    To address these challenges, alternative clustering techniques like DBSCAN and Hierarchical Clustering should be considered. DBSCAN is particularly useful for datasets with irregularly shaped clusters and varying densities, while Hierarchical Clustering offers the advantage of flexibility, allowing the user to choose the number of clusters at different levels of the hierarchy based on a dendrogram.

    Additionally, techniques such as dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) can improve clustering performance, particularly when working with high-dimensional data. By reducing the number of features, these methods can help uncover clearer cluster structures and enhance the accuracy of the clustering results.

    In conclusion, the clustering results suggest that K-Means is not the most effective approach for this dataset. Exploring algorithms like DBSCAN or Hierarchical Clustering, and utilizing dimensionality reduction techniques, could provide more accurate and meaningful clusters, offering better insights and improved decision-making capabilities.
